# LOG 002 — Arrival at Kepler
I did not expect to cry.
I have run the orbital approach simulation forty-seven times over the last fourteen months. I know what Kepler-1649c looks like on a screen. I have studied its spectral data, its atmospheric composition reports, its tidal lock models. I have memorized its day side and its night side and the thin, miraculous band of temperate climate that wraps around its terminator like a ring of possibility.
None of that prepared me for the viewport.
First Contact
It appeared at 0314 ship time, a pale smear at the edge of the dark that slowly resolved itself into something real. Something with texture. Rust-colored highlands sweeping into deep greens I can only describe as alive. Cloud systems spiraling across the northern hemisphere with the lazy confidence of weather that has been happening for billions of years and intends to keep happening long after I am gone.
I pressed my hand against the cold glass and just looked at it for a long time.
ARIA, my navigation AI, offered three times to begin the orbital insertion sequence. I told her to wait. She did not argue. I think she has learned, over these fourteen months, when to leave me alone with something.
What the Data Cannot Carry
The scientists back home have a word for planets like this: habitable zone. It is a clinical phrase, a distance calculation, a Goldilocks equation. It does not carry the weight of what it actually means to see one hanging in the void after months of nothing but stars and your own reflection.
Habitable zone means: the right distance from the right star. It means: liquid water is possible. It means: you will not instantly die here.
What it does not mean, and what no amount of data can mean, is this — this particular shade of green, this specific spiral of cloud, this precise sense of vertigo when you realize that somewhere on that surface, given enough time and enough luck and enough of whatever it is that turns chemistry into biology, something might have already found a way to breathe.
The Science Begins Tomorrow
Orbital insertion is complete. I am 800 kilometers above the surface and everything is nominal. ARIA has already flagged seventeen landing site candidates in the temperate band. The atmospheric sensors are running their first full composition sweep. The spectrometer is tasting the light reflecting off those green highlands and whispering its findings to me in numbers I will spend weeks interpreting.
Tomorrow I begin the real work. Surface surveys. Sample collection protocols. The long, methodical process of turning wonder into data.
Tonight, though, I am just going to sit here at the viewport.
Some things deserve to be felt before they are measured.
