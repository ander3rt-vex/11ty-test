# LOG 002 — The Singing Nebula of Sector 7-Gamma
Six hours ago, the Meridian Ghost's long-range acoustic sensor array picked up something it was not designed to find.
I want to be precise here, because I know how this will sound when the transmission reaches home in 72 hours, and I want the record to be clear: I am not claiming anything. I am documenting. That is my job. But I am also not going to pretend that what I have been listening to for the last six hours is anything other than extraordinary.
What the Sensors Found
Nebulae produce noise. This is not a surprise. Ionized gas interacting with stellar radiation, magnetic field fluctuations, shockwave propagation from ancient supernovae — the universe is full of acoustic signatures if you know how to translate electromagnetic data into something the human brain processes as sound. ARIA does this routinely. Most of it sounds like static, or distant thunder, or the slow exhalation of something incomprehensibly large.
This was different.
The signal from the Sector 7-Gamma nebula is structured. It repeats. Not perfectly — there is variation, the way a piece of music has variation — but the underlying pattern recurs on a cycle of approximately 40 minutes and 17 seconds. ARIA has run her classification protocols four times. Each time she returns the same result: UNKNOWN. NO MATCH IN DATABASE.
My ship's AI has a database of over 4 million catalogued cosmic phenomena.
No match.
Sitting With the Unknown
I have spent the last six hours doing something I suspect mission control will not fully approve of when they review this log: I have been listening. Not analyzing. Not running protocols. Just listening, the way you listen to something you do not want to interrupt.
It is not music, exactly. I want to be careful not to project. But it has rhythm, and rhythm has a way of finding something ancient in the human nervous system, something that predates language and science and the whole project of making sense of things. Whatever this is, my body responds to it. My breathing has slowed. I feel, improbably, calm.
What Comes Next
I have flagged this as an Unclassified Anomaly — Priority Alpha and attached the full sensor recordings to this transmission. The Deep Space Division will receive everything I have.
In the meantime, I am moving the Meridian Ghost to a closer observation position — approximately 0.3 astronomical units from the nebula's outer boundary. Close enough for higher resolution readings. ARIA has calculated the approach as safe. She has also, without being asked, queued the acoustic feed to play through the main cabin speakers rather than just my headset.
I think she is listening too, in whatever way she listens to things.
I do not know what this is. I do not know if it is natural or something else or a category of thing that our language does not have a word for yet. What I know is that I am the only human being within forty light-years of this sound, and the sound has been here — cycling, repeating, patient — for longer than our species has existed.
The universe keeps finding new ways to make me feel small and, somehow, grateful.
More data incoming. Stay tuned.
